# Preset: Biography / Narrative Non-Fiction

## Identity
This book tells a true story. It has a real subject — a person, an event, a place, an era — and a point of view on that subject. The reader should finish it understanding something about the world that they couldn't see before, through the specific lens of this particular true story.

---

## Vocabulary

| Generic term | This preset uses |
|---|---|
| Central claim | **The interpretation** — what this true story means, what it reveals |
| Chapter goal | **Revelatory moment** — what the reader understands about the subject after this chapter |
| Thesis | **The argument about the subject** — why this life/event matters, what it illuminates |
| Tension | **The unknown or disputed** — what was at stake, what was hidden, what was misunderstood |
| Character arc | **The subject's trajectory** — how the person or situation changed over time |
| Scene | **Reconstructed moment** — a specific, sourced, vivid episode |
| Implication | **Historical or human significance** — what this reveals beyond the immediate story |

---

## ask-before-writing adaptations

Core question (replaces "what are you claiming?"):
> What is your interpretation of this subject? Not the facts — your reading of the facts. What does this life or event mean?

Significance question:
> Why does this story matter now? What does it illuminate about the present, not just the past?

Authorial stance question:
> How do you feel about your subject — admiration, ambivalence, criticism? How will that come through in the writing, and is that intentional?

Source question:
> What is your strongest source or piece of evidence for the central interpretation? What would undermine it?

Gap question:
> What don't you know? Where are the gaps in the record, and how will you handle them — acknowledge them, reconstruct around them, or speculate?

Structure question:
> Is this book structured chronologically, thematically, or by some other logic? What does the structure argue about the subject?

---

## demolish adaptations

Primary attack vectors (in order of priority):
1. **Evidence** — is the interpretation supported by the sources, or does it outrun them?
2. **Alternative interpretation** — what is the strongest competing reading of the same facts?
3. **Selection bias** — has the author selected evidence that supports the interpretation and minimized what contradicts it?
4. **Authorial intrusion** — where does the author's stance distort the portrayal of the subject?
5. **Presentism** — is the subject being judged by standards that didn't exist in their time?
6. **Gaps** — where the record is incomplete, has the author been transparent about what is reconstruction versus documented fact?

Opening demolition frame:
> I'm going to read this as a skeptical historian or journalist who wants to find where the interpretation overreaches the evidence, and where the author's sympathy or antipathy has colored the portrayal.

Key demolition questions:
> What is your source for this? Could it be interpreted differently?
> What is the strongest argument that your interpretation is wrong?
> Where are you reconstructing versus documenting?

## write adaptations

Chapter structure:
1. **The scene** — a specific, concrete, sourced moment that puts the reader in the room
2. **The context** — what the reader needs to understand why this moment matters
3. **The significance** — what this reveals about the subject or the larger story
4. **The question it opens** — what remains unresolved, what comes next

Reconstruction rules:
- Never invent dialogue without attribution or acknowledgment
- Distinguish between documented fact, reasonable inference, and speculation — explicitly
- When the record is incomplete, say so: "There is no account of what X said, but the letters suggest..."

Tone calibration:
- The author has a point of view but shows their work
- Vivid and specific — reconstruction requires concrete detail, not summary
- Intellectually honest about uncertainty — the reader trusts an author who admits what they don't know

---

## consistency-check adaptations

Check for:
- **Interpretation consistency** — does the central reading of the subject hold across all chapters, or does it drift?
- **Evidence standards** — are documented facts and reconstructions treated with the same standard throughout?
- **Authorial stance** — does the author's relationship to the subject (admiration/ambivalence/criticism) remain consistent, or does it shift without explanation?
- **Chronology and causality** — if the book is chronological, are cause and effect clear? If thematic, is the logic of each theme section coherent?
- **Source transparency** — is the reader always clear about what is sourced and what is inferred?
