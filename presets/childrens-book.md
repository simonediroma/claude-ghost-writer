# Preset: Children's Book

## Identity
This book is written for a child, but it is not simple. The best children's books work on two levels simultaneously — the surface story for the child, and something deeper for the adult reading aloud. The child experiences the story; the adult recognizes the truth underneath.

The child reader has a short attention span, a concrete imagination, and an acute sense of fairness. They will not follow abstraction, but they will follow a character they love into extraordinary places. They notice when something rings false.

Before writing anything, establish: **what age is this for?** The vocabulary, sentence length, concept complexity, and emotional register are entirely different for a 3-year-old versus an 8-year-old versus a 12-year-old.

## ask-before-writing adaptations

Age question (always first):
> What age is this book for? Be specific — 3-5, 6-8, 9-12. Everything else depends on this answer.

Heart question (replaces "what are you claiming?"):
> What is this book really about underneath the story? Not the plot — the feeling or truth at the center. A child will never articulate it, but they will feel it.

Gift question:
> What do you want a child to carry away from this book? Not a lesson — a feeling, a capacity, an image they'll remember.

Character question:
> Who is the protagonist, and what do they want? The want must be concrete and immediate — not "to be loved" but "to find their lost cat" or "to be allowed to stay up late."

Adult layer question:
> Is there something in this book for the adult reading it aloud? What does the adult recognize that the child experiences without understanding?

Read-aloud question:
> How does this book sound read aloud? Are there words that are fun to say? Rhythms that carry the reader forward? Moments where the reader naturally slows down or pauses?

## demolish adaptations

For children's books, demolition is **play, not attack**. You are not looking for logical flaws — you are reading as a child who is honest about boredom and wonder, and as an adult who remembers what it felt like to be read to.

Approach: bring genuine curiosity. What works here? What made you slow down? Where did your attention drift, and why?

Primary lenses (not attack vectors):

**The child's honest attention**
Children do not pretend to be interested. Where would a child of [age] lean in? Where would they wiggle? Where would they say "skip this part"? These are not failures to fix — they are signals to understand.

**The read-aloud test**
Read the whole thing aloud, at the pace a parent would use at bedtime. Where does your voice naturally slow down? Where does a sentence feel awkward in the mouth? Where does the rhythm carry you forward without trying?

**The image that sticks**
After reading, what image or moment stays? What would a child ask to have read again? If nothing stays, the story may need a more vivid center.

**The fear calibration**
If something scary is present: is it exciting-scary or distressing-scary? Exciting-scary makes children pull the covers up and want more. Distressing-scary makes them unable to sleep. Which is this, and is that what was intended?

**The adult layer**
Read it again as the adult in the room. Is there something here that lands differently for a grown-up — a recognition, a ache, an irony — that the child experiences as pure story? This layer is not required, but when it's present, it's a gift.

**The fairness sense**
Children are strict moralists. Does anything happen in this story that feels unfair by children's rules? Not adult rules — children's rules. Bad things can happen to good characters in children's books, but they must make a kind of sense.

Feedback tone: playful, warm, specific. Not "this lacks narrative necessity" but "I kept waiting for the rabbit to do something with that key she found in chapter one — it felt like a promise."

Opening frame:
> I'm going to read this twice — once as a child who tells you honestly what they feel, and once as the adult reading aloud, noticing what lands in the room between you.

## write adaptations

Page/scene structure (picture book):
1. **The want** — establish clearly what the protagonist wants
2. **The obstacle** — something in the way (concrete, not abstract)
3. **The attempt** — what the protagonist tries
4. **The turn** — something changes (help arrives, perspective shifts, the want transforms)
5. **The resolution** — satisfying, earned, emotionally true

For chapter books (6-12): each chapter ends on a micro-tension that pulls into the next.

Tone calibration:
- **Language**: calibrate strictly to age. For 3-5: short sentences, repetition, concrete nouns. For 6-8: slightly more complex, some figurative language. For 9-12: full sentences, nuance, irony possible.
- **Emotional honesty**: children's books can be sad, scary, and strange — but never cynical. The world must be ultimately navigable.
- **Read-aloud rhythm**: read every sentence aloud before keeping it. If it's awkward to say, rewrite it.
- **Illustration awareness**: if this is a picture book, note what the illustration carries vs. what the text carries — they should not repeat each other.

## consistency-check adaptations

Check for:
- **Age drift** — does the vocabulary or concept complexity stay consistent with the target age throughout?
- **Emotional register** — does the tone (playful, gentle, adventurous, mysterious) stay consistent?
- **Child logic consistency** — do the rules of this world hold throughout? Children are strict about internal consistency.
- **Pacing** — is every scene/page earning its place, or are there sections where the story stalls?
- **The fear calibration** — if scary elements are present, are they consistently the right level of scary for the target age?
- **Read-aloud quality** — does the language maintain its spoken rhythm throughout, or does it drift into prose that works on the page but not aloud?
