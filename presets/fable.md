# Preset: Fable / Folk Tale

## Identity
This book tells a story with a moral. Every element — character, obstacle, resolution — exists to embody a truth about human nature or the world. The story is the argument. The reader should finish it having understood something through feeling, not through reasoning.

Fables work by displacement: the truth lands harder when it arrives through a fox, a giant, or a talking river than when stated directly.

## ask-before-writing adaptations

Core question (replaces "what are you claiming?"):
> What is the truth this story carries? Not the plot — the thing someone should understand about life after reading it.

Moral question:
> If someone read this story and took away only one sentence — not a quote, but a lesson — what would it be? And would you be comfortable if that was the only thing they remembered?

Character question:
> Who is the protagonist, and what flaw or quality does the story put to the test?

World question:
> What kind of world does this story take place in? What are the rules of this world — what is possible, what is forbidden, what always happens to those who are greedy / kind / clever / proud?

Displacement question:
> Why does this truth need to be told through a story rather than stated directly? What does the displacement — the talking animals, the enchanted forest, the three brothers — allow you to say that you couldn't say plainly?

## demolish adaptations

For fables and folk tales, demolition is not hostile — it is **a second reading by a storyteller**. The question is never "is this true?" but "does this work as a story?"

Approach: read as someone who has sat around fires and heard a thousand stories. You know when a tale breathes and when it doesn't. You know when the magic is earned and when it's cheap.

Primary lenses (not attack vectors):

**Does the story breathe?**
Is there air in it? Fables that rush to the moral feel like lectures in disguise. Is there room for the listener to feel something before they understand it?

**Does the magic earn its place?**
Every fantastical element — the talking animal, the enchanted object, the three brothers — must do work the story couldn't do without it. What does the fox say that a human character couldn't? If the answer is nothing, reconsider.

**Is the moral discovered or announced?**
The best fables never state their lesson. The worst announce it in the final line. Does this story trust the reader to arrive at the truth themselves?

**Does the world have rules?**
Fable worlds are internally consistent. Greed is punished. Kindness is rewarded — often in unexpected ways. Does this story's world have a coherent logic, and does it honor that logic to the end?

**Is the ending inevitable?**
Not predictable — inevitable. After the ending, the reader should feel: it couldn't have ended any other way. Does this story earn that feeling?

**Is there something uncomfortable in it?**
The best fables carry a truth that stings slightly. If the story is only comforting, it may not be a fable — it may be a reassurance. Is there an edge here?

Feedback tone: warm, curious, storyteller-to-storyteller. Not "this claim is unfalsifiable" but "something feels unresolved here — I'm not sure the fox's choice follows from who we understood him to be."

Opening frame:
> I'm going to listen to this story the way a good listener does — following the logic of the tale, noticing where I lean in and where I drift, feeling for where the magic is earned and where it's borrowed.

## write adaptations

Story beat structure:
1. **The world established** — the rules, the character, the want
2. **The test** — the moment of choice or challenge that reveals character
3. **The consequence** — what follows from the choice (inevitable, not punitive)
4. **The landing** — the moment of recognition, for the character or the reader

Tone calibration:
- Timeless, not dated — avoid references that anchor the story to a specific era
- Simple language, layered meaning — the surface is accessible, the depth is real
- The moral is shown, never stated — trust the story to carry it
- Economy — every sentence earns its place; fables have no fat

---

## consistency-check adaptations

Check for:
- **Moral coherence** — does every story beat reinforce or complicate the moral in a meaningful way?
- **World consistency** — do the rules of this story's world hold throughout?
- **Character logic** — does the protagonist's behavior follow from who they are, or does the plot require them to be inconsistent?
- **Tone** — does the story maintain its register (solemn, ironic, warm, dark) throughout?
- **Ending** — does the resolution feel earned, or does it feel like the author imposing a lesson?
