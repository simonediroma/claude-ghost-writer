# Preset: Novel / Fiction

## Identity
This book tells a story. It has characters who want things, encounter obstacles, and change (or refuse to change). The reader should finish it having felt something — and having understood something about human experience that they couldn't have learned any other way.

---

## Vocabulary

| Generic term | This preset uses |
|---|---|
| Central claim | **Central dramatic question** — what must be resolved for the story to end? |
| Chapter goal | **Scene objective** — what changes in this scene? |
| Reader transformation | **Emotional journey** — what does the reader feel and understand by the end? |
| Thesis | **Theme** — the truth about human experience this story embodies |
| Argument | **Dramatic tension** — the force that drives scenes forward |
| Implication | **Consequence** — what follows from this character's choice? |
| Structure | **Arc** — the shape of change over time |

---

## ask-before-writing adaptations

Core question (replaces "what are you claiming?"):
> What is the central dramatic question of this book? The question that must be answered for the story to feel complete?

Theme question (replaces "what is your thesis?"):
> What truth about human experience does this story embody? Not the plot — the meaning underneath it.

Character question:
> Who changes in this book, and what do they have to lose or give up to change?

Structure question:
> What is the worst moment for your protagonist — the point of maximum pressure? Where does it fall in the book?

Stakes question:
> What happens if the central dramatic question is never resolved? What does the protagonist lose?

Implications question (replaces logical implications):
> What does this character's choice in this scene make inevitable? What doors close?

---

## demolish adaptations

Primary attack vectors (in order of priority):
1. **Motivation** — does the character's action follow from who they are, or does the plot require it?
2. **Consequence** — do choices have real consequences, or does the story reset?
3. **Theme coherence** — do the events of the book actually embody the stated theme?
4. **Earned change** — if the character changes, has the book laid the groundwork for it?
5. **Scene necessity** — does this scene change something, or is it description?

Opening demolition frame:
> I'm going to read this as a reader who wants to be moved but won't accept shortcuts. I'll attack any moment where the story asks me to feel something it hasn't earned.

Key demolition questions:
> Why does [character] do this? Is it because of who they are, or because the plot needs them to?
> What changes in this scene? If I removed it, what would be different?
> Has the book earned this moment emotionally?

---

## write adaptations

Scene structure:
1. **Entry point** — where are we, who wants what?
2. **Pressure** — what force opposes or complicates what the character wants?
3. **Turn** — the moment something changes (a decision, a revelation, a failure)
4. **Exit point** — where are we now? What is different from the entry?

Every scene must change something: a relationship, a belief, a plan, a power balance. A scene that ends where it started is not a scene — it is description.

Tone calibration:
- Show, don't explain
- Trust the reader to feel without being told what to feel
- Specificity over generality — one concrete detail beats three abstract ones

---

## consistency-check adaptations

Check for:
- **Character consistency** — do characters act according to their established psychology, or according to plot convenience?
- **Consequence tracking** — do earlier choices have visible effects in later chapters?
- **Tone drift** — does the narrative voice shift register unexpectedly?
- **Theme coherence** — do the events of the book add up to the stated theme, or do they contradict it?
- **Pacing** — do scenes alternate between tension and release, or does the pressure flatline?
