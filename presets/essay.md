# Preset: Essay / Argumentative Non-Fiction

## Identity
This book makes an argument. It has a central claim that is either true or false. The reader should finish it believing something they didn't believe before, or understanding something they didn't understand before.

---

## Vocabulary

| Generic term | This preset uses |
|---|---|
| Central claim | **Thesis** — the specific proposition the book argues |
| Chapter goal | **Argument** — what this chapter proves |
| Reader transformation | **Position shift** — what the reader now believes |
| Tension | **Objection** — the strongest counter-argument |
| Character arc | **Conceptual development** — how the argument builds |
| Scene | **Section** — a unit of argument |
| Inciting incident | **The problem** — what makes this thesis necessary |

---

## ask-before-writing adaptations

Core question:
> What exactly are you claiming? Not the topic — the claim. What is true according to your text that would not be true without it?

Push for falsifiability:
> What evidence or argument would prove you wrong?

Implications question:
> If your thesis is true, what does that make impossible or inevitable for the reader?

Structure question:
> What does the reader need to accept first in order to accept the final thesis?

---

## demolish adaptations

Primary attack vectors (in order of priority):
1. **Falsifiability** — is the thesis testable? Could anything prove it wrong?
2. **Circularity** — does the argument use the conclusion as a premise?
3. **Scope** — does the thesis prove what it claims, or something weaker?
4. **Missing foundations** — what does the argument assume without proving?
5. **Strongest objection** — what is the best case against this thesis?

Opening demolition frame:
> I'm going to attack this argument as a hostile but intelligent reader who finds your thesis interesting but wants to find the holes before they commit to believing it.

---

## write adaptations

Section structure:
1. **The objection or problem** — open with the strongest resistance to this section's argument
2. **The argument** — the claim and its support
3. **The implication** — what follows if this is true
4. **The bridge** — how this section's conclusion becomes the next section's premise

Tone calibration:
- Intellectual but not academic
- Confident but not dismissive of objections
- The author has a position and defends it without becoming a lecturer

---

## consistency-check adaptations

Check for:
- **Thesis drift** — does the central thesis shift meaning between chapters?
- **Unresolved objections** — were counter-arguments raised but never answered?
- **Argument gaps** — does each chapter's conclusion actually support the next chapter's premise?
- **Tone consistency** — does the author maintain their argumentative voice or drift into description?
